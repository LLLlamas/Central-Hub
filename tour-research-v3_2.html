The rider.

The artist's contract addendum: technical and hospitality requirements the venue must fulfill. Legally binding, typically 5–15 pages. Where most version-control disasters happen — and where the hub can win biggest. Tap any section to expand.

Sample Tech & Hospitality Rider

REV 8 · LIVE LINK

01 Cover & preamble

+

The opening page. Who's playing, who to call, what era of the rider this is.

  • Artist name & logo — bold, top of page
  • Revision number & date — critical; old revs cause real problems
  • Contact block — TM (tour manager), PM (production manager), FOH (front-of-house) engineer, backline tech (cell + email)
  • Genre & reference — link to a recording so the house engineer has a sonic reference
  • Travel party size — people, buses, trucks
  • Binding statement — "this rider is part of the contract"

02 Stage plot

+

Bird's-eye diagram of the stage. Where every player stands, every instrument lives, every mic goes. Most-referenced page on show day.

  • Band positions — "Lead Vox," "Guitar 1," "Drums," oriented from performer's POV
  • Backline placement — amps, cabs, drum kit, keys, to scale
  • Monitor positions — wedges numbered ("Mix 1 — Lead Vox")
  • Mics & DI boxes — type and location (DI = direct input, for plugging instruments straight into the console)
  • Risers — drum, keys, dimensions noted
  • AC drops — where power lands
  • Cable runs — for tidiness and trip safety
  • Sub-snake locations — stage boxes for fast cabling
  • Audience direction — clearly marked so nobody flips the plot

03 Input list

+

Numbered list of every audio source into the console. Arena rock: 40–80 inputs. Pop with playback: 120+.

  • Channel number — sequential, never skip
  • Source — "Kick In," "Snare Top," "Lead Vox"
  • Mic preference — Beta 91A, C414, e906
  • Stand — boom, short, clip, none
  • 48V phantom — on/off (critical for condensers)
  • Insert / outboard — gates, comps, EQs
  • Stage box assignment — which sub-snake
  • Notes — "Ride is left of kit," "Keys are stereo"

04 Front-of-house (FOH)

+

What the audience-mix engineer needs from the back of the room.

  • Console — DiGiCo SD12, Avid S6L, Yamaha CL5 (with acceptable alternates)
  • PA (the main speaker system) — d&b, L-Acoustics, Meyer; SPL (sound pressure level) targets; coverage
  • Subs — quantity, config (cardioid, end-fire), placement
  • Outboard — reverbs, delays, comps
  • FOH position — in the audience field, never under a balcony
  • Platform size — 10×10 minimum; larger for cameras
  • Talkback to stage — to monitor world

05 Monitors

+

Everything the band needs to hear themselves. Essentially a separate console world.

  • Monitor console — usually matched to FOH
  • Wedges — number, model, mix assignment
  • IEMs (in-ear monitors — wireless earpieces the band wears) — Shure PSM or Sennheiser; number of mixes
  • Drum-fill / side-fills — for stereo stage coverage
  • RF coordination — frequencies, antenna placement, scan time
  • Talkback to FOH — for the monitor engineer

06 Lighting

+

Often a separate document. Owned by the LD.

  • Light plot — fixture types and truss positions
  • Front / back wash — color and intensity targets
  • Movers — quantity, brand, positions
  • Followspots — number, throw distance, position
  • Console — grandMA, Hog, ChamSys
  • Haze / fog — required for beam visibility
  • Power — total wattage, distro needs
  • Truss height — minimum ceiling clearance

07 Rigging

+

Where weight hangs from the ceiling. Approved by the venue's rigger before the truck rolls in.

  • Hang points — coordinates on a venue plan
  • Point loads — weight per point (lbs/kg)
  • Total load — within venue's certified capacity
  • Motor count & chain length — chain motors that fly the rig
  • Bridles — when beams don't align with desired points
  • Trim heights — final hung height per truss

08 Backline split

+

Two lists: gear the tour brings, gear the venue must provide. The most-edited section between revs.

  • Drums — kit, sizes, hardware, throne, cymbals
  • Guitar amps — Fender, Marshall, Mesa, Vox; specific models
  • Bass rig — head and cab
  • Keys — Nord, Yamaha; stand requirements
  • Risers — dimensions
  • Shared items — cymbal stands, mic stands, music stands
  • Substitutions — clearly marked as OK or not

09 Power

+

Crucial when the tour brings its own PA, lights, and video. Confirmed before trucks roll in.

  • Service amperage — 200A, 400A, 600A, three-phase
  • Distribution — Cam-Lok, Powerlock; disconnect location
  • Phase balance — even loading across legs
  • Generator backup — required outdoors
  • Stage drops — quad boxes for backline
  • Isolated audio power — separate from lighting to avoid hum

10 Local labor

+

Number of stagehands the venue must call, by department, with start times. Drives a chunk of show cost.

  • Riggers — 4–8 for arena, work first
  • Loaders — cases off and on trucks
  • Audio hands — PA setup
  • Lighting hands — truss build, fixture hang
  • Video hands — LED wall assembly
  • Backline hands — wheel cases, set drums
  • Stewards / push crew — changeover and scenic
  • Followspot ops — for the show

11 Schedule & load-in

+

Proposed running order. Venue PM may push back. Final version becomes the day sheet.

  • Truck arrival — earliest hour
  • Load-in start — when riggers begin
  • Build phases — rigging, audio, lights, video
  • Soundcheck — 60–90 min window
  • Doors — public entry
  • Opener set — start, length, changeover
  • Headliner set — start, length, encore
  • Curfew — hard stop
  • Load-out — completion target

12 Dressing rooms

+

Where the rider shifts from technical to personal.

  • Number of rooms — artist, band, crew, support, production office
  • Furnishings — couches, tables, mirrors, hangers
  • Climate — temperature targets
  • Towels — clean white, specific count
  • Showers — hot water, accessible
  • Wi-Fi — credentials provided
  • Signage — directions backstage
  • Production office — desks, printer, internet

13 Food & beverage

+

Two crew meals daily on show days, plus dressing room snacks and drinks.

  • Crew lunch & dinner — count, time windows, hot food, veg/vegan options
  • Dietary — gluten-free, dairy-free, allergies, kosher, halal
  • Dressing-room snacks — fruit, nuts, bars, deli platters
  • Beverages — water, coffee, tea, soda, sometimes alcohol
  • Post-show meal — for the band after
  • Buy-out — cash per person if catering can't accommodate

14 Passes & credentials

+

Who gets what level of access. Printed laminates with photos on big tours.

  • All Access (AAA) — anywhere, any time
  • Working pass — backstage during work hours
  • VIP / meet-and-greet — limited pre-show access (VIP = very important person ticket package)
  • Crew laminates — by department
  • Press / photo — pit-only, often time-limited
  • Counts per pass type — how many of each

15 Parking, security, logistics

+

Unglamorous but essential. Determines whether load-in goes smoothly.

  • Truck parking — count, shore power
  • Bus parking — shore power, water, dump access
  • Fuel — nearby diesel, gas station distance
  • Truck routing — turning radius, height, dock height
  • Security plan — barricade, pit security, t-shirt count
  • Runner — local hire who drives crew on errands
  • Hospital — nearest ER location

16 Settlement

+

Often a separate doc. Terms for end-of-night settlement.

  • Deal terms — flat, percentage, or door deal
  • Ticket scaling — price tiers, holds, comps
  • Approved expenses — what's artist's vs promoter's
  • Settlement timing — usually 30–45 min after curfew
  • Payment method — wire, certified check, cash
  • Tax docs — W-9, withholding for foreign acts

The biggest pain in the rider workflow is version drift. Artist updates rev 7. PM emails rev 7 to the venue. Venue's lighting sub still has rev 5. Show day, three versions of the truth show up. Whoever owns the rider's live link wins this category.

How it's stored today

Dropbox folders

PM keeps a folder per tour. Tech rider PDF, stage plot PDF, input list spreadsheet, hospitality Word doc. All emailed as attachments. New version goes in the folder; old versions don't get deleted.

Where it should live

One live link per show

Every show has a URL or QR code that always points to the current rider, plot, and input list. PM updates rev 9, holders see rev 9 next time they open it.

What this unlocks

Send once, update forever

Advance email goes out at week 8 with the link. Week 4, artist tweaks inputs. Week 1, truck count changes. Show day, venue opens the link, gets latest. No re-sends. No "which version?" calls.

Five phases.

Each has its own source of truth. Hand-offs between phases are where info gets lost. A good hub owns the hand-offs.

Phase 01 · Booking

Agent sells the shows

When: 12 → 6 months out

Source of truth: deal memos + agent's routing spreadsheet

Output: dates, venues, capacities, guarantees, splits

Pain: emailed spreadsheet → manually retyped into the tour app. First lossy hand-off.

Phase 02 · Advance

PM prepares each show

When: 8–12 weeks out, intensifies at 2

Source of truth: advance email threads + latest rider PDF

What: tour PM contacts every venue. Sends rider, plot, input list. Confirms trucks, parking, labor, power, rigging.

Pain: rider versioning. Old PDFs float around. Showvella and RiderForge exist because the big apps don't fix this.

Phase 03 · Production rehearsals

Build the show

When: 1–4 weeks before kickoff

Source of truth: the rehearsal room itself

What: full production loads into a soundstage or arena. Lighting cues programmed, video timecode locked, IEM mixes dialed, automation choreographed.

Pain: tribal knowledge generated here never makes it into any system.

Phase 04 · Show day

Day sheet runs the day

When: every day on the road, for 4–18 months

Source of truth: day sheet (one per role, customized)

Contents: show clock, crew calls, catering, dressing rooms, contacts, Wi-Fi, hospital

Reality: artist's day sheet has press/hotel/security details audio crew shouldn't see. Audio's has stagehand calls artist doesn't need.

Phase 05 · Settlement

Money gets reconciled

When: every night → end of tour

Source of truth: box office sheet + tour accounting Excel

What: ticket counts, walk-up, comps, fees, splits, taxes. Cash deposited. Per diems paid. Crew payroll. End of tour: full P&L.

Reality: most tour accounting still lives in Excel. Wide open.

The insight

Win the hand-offs

Most apps focus on phases 3–4. The friction users feel is at the seams: booking→advance, advance→rehearsal, show day→settlement.

A hub that ingests cleanly from phase 1, preserves data through phase 2, captures phase 3 decisions, runs phase 4 well, and exports cleanly to phase 5 wins on a different dimension than feature parity.

Apps in detail.

Master Tour is the incumbent everyone loves to hate but can't replace. Daysheets is the modern challenger taking serious share. Niche apps fill the gaps.

Master Tour

by Eventric · founded 2009
Incumbent Industry standard

What it is

The long-standing standard for arena and stadium tours. Used by Beyoncé, Florida Georgia Line, Fleetwood Mac, Disney on Ice, Harlem Globetrotters. Desktop app (Mac/Windows/Linux) for the TM and PM to build the tour. Free mobile app where everyone else views it — 100,000+ users, 55,000+ monthly active.

Structure

Organization → Tour → Tour Date → Event. Inside an Org you create Tours. Inside a Tour, you create Tour Dates. Each Date is one of three types: Show Day, Day Off, or Travel Day. Show Days have an Event attached — the actual gig at a Venue.

Building a tour

The Setup Wizard walks the TM through a date range. For each date: pick a Day Type. For Show Days, search the Venue Database (15,000+ venues). Found → name, address, phone, URL auto-fill. Not found → Google search fallback. Last resort → manual create. Schedule Templates pre-fill typical times.

Schedule section

The heart of the app. Each Schedule Item has Title, Start/End Time, Day Of / Day After flag (for post-midnight items), Status (Unconfirmed grey → Confirmed blue → Done green), Time Display style, and Visibility controlling who sees it. Travel Items show up in the Schedule too — a separate Travel tab handles flights, drives, rail, sea, with Travel Party assignments (A Party, B Party).

Advance section

Separate from Schedule. Each Show gets an Events Tab (local record: dimensions, contacts, production info) and an Advance Tab (customizable checklist). You design an Advance Template with any fields you need (rigging capacity, dock height, parking, runner contact). Anything already in Venue or Schedule auto-fills. Status sidebar shows progress per date. Templates save at Org level and reuse across tours. Custom fields merge into print templates for day sheets.

Print & export

Print Templates generate day sheets, tour books, advance docs. User builds a layout with Print Blocks (Venue Block, Schedule Block, Travel Block) and Merge Fields. Any date can produce a multi-page PDF. This is where the daily posted day sheet comes from.

Mobile companion

Free, read-only for crew, limited editing for Managers/Admins. Long-press any item to copy details to clipboard. Share icon copies an entire day. Tags are a Yelp-for-touring layer (best coffee in Seattle, best guitar tech in Biloxi). FlightAware integrates for live flight tracking. TripIt imports travel.

Where data comes from

  • Routing: manually entered, date by date
  • Venues: auto-fill from MT's 15,000+ DB, fallback to Google
  • Personnel: 150,000+ records — search and add
  • Travel: manual + TripIt import + FlightAware tracking
  • Schedule: manual + reusable templates
  • Riders: attached files (PDF, image), not structured data
  • Advance: custom forms filled by hand or via emailed back-and-forth
Strengths
  • Mature, comprehensive — accounting, guest list, set lists, venue DB
  • 15,000+ venue database is a real moat
  • Real-time co-editing across desktop and mobile
  • Granular visibility on Schedule, Travel, Hotels, Tasks, Crew
  • Offline mode actually works
  • Powerful print template system
  • Tags create a community knowledge base
Weaknesses
  • Recent UI overhaul widely disliked
  • Attachments slow / unreliable
  • Desktop-first feels dated in 2026
  • Steep learning curve
  • Almost no AI / smart ingest
  • Riders are flat attachments — no versioning
  • Per-admin licensing gets expensive

Daysheets

by Daysheets, Inc. · modern challenger
Modern challenger AI-forward

What it is

The new player taking real share, especially with younger TMs. Mobile-first (iOS native, Mac native for tour creation; Android trails). Reviews are unusually positive: "far and away better than competitors" is a typical line. Founders ship updates frequently and respond to user feedback.

Structure

Organization → Tour → Day → Personnel + Reservations. Orgs group tours, billing, permissions. Each tour has Personnel (with Groups and Group Tags), Routing, Schedules, Flights, Transfers (bus/ferry/train), Lodging. Each day gets a Day Type (show, off, travel, rehearsal, promo, hold) — fully customizable with custom colors.

Groups & Group Tags — the killer feature

This is where Daysheets pulls ahead. Groups are travel parties (A Party = artist circle, B Party = band + key crew, Crew Party = production). When a party exceeds 10 people, split into groups for personalized itineraries.

Group Tags are sub-groups inside a parent. Inside Crew Party: "Lighting," "Video," "Carps." Each tag gets its own filtered view. Advanced Group Tags pull people across parents — a "Production" tag could include the road manager from B Party, the production coord from Crew Party, the TM from A Party.

Toggle on "Request Information from Individuals Added to this Group" and every new person gets prompted for passport, frequent flyer, dietary, emergency contact. Data populates without the TM asking twice.

Adding personnel — three ways

"Add Personnel" opens a drawer with three options:

  • Search or create — type a name. If they've toured with you before, they appear. If not, create with name + role (only required fields). Toggle on Travel Profile to request data from them.
  • Import from CSV — drop a spreadsheet. Daysheets walks you through column mapping. If your CSV has a Group column, people auto-assign to groups. Preview lets you toggle rows on/off before importing.
  • Import from previous tour — the killer feature. Pick a past tour, import roster, groups, tags, permissions, visibility — or just selected people. New tour set up in 10 minutes instead of 10 hours.

Permissions & Visibility

Each person gets a Permission level (Read Only, Edit, Custom) and Visibility scope (All Events, Group-Only, Custom). Group defaults apply when you assign someone, but you override per individual. That's why Daysheets handles "the read-only A-Party member who needs C-Party insight" cleanly.

Daysheets AI — flight import is the demo

Drop up to 100 flight itinerary PDFs into the app. AI parses departure/arrival airports, dates, times, flight numbers, passengers, record locators, seats. Matches passengers to existing personnel by name (with a "legal flying name" field for mismatches like "Mike" vs "Michael Anthony Smith"). Unmatched names go in a Guest Passengers pile you can swap.

Each flight enters in "ready for review" state. You eyeball parsed data side-by-side with the original, fix anything wrong, click Import. It lands in the right Day and shows up in the Flight Grid.

Schedule templates

Reusable day-shapes. A typed list of items (Bus Call, Lobby, Load-in, Soundcheck) with times. Apply to any new show day → instant population. Multiple templates: Arena, Theater, Festival, Promo Day.

Where data comes from

  • Routing: Tour Builder, manual
  • Personnel: CSV import or "import from previous tour" — much faster than MT
  • Travel profiles: auto-requested from individuals
  • Flights: drag-and-drop AI parsing — the headline feature
  • Hotels & transfers: manual
  • Schedules: templates + per-day edits
Strengths
  • Best-in-class mobile UX (especially iOS)
  • Group Tags model — deepest permission system in the category
  • Daysheets AI flight import: drag a PDF, get data
  • "Import from previous tour" makes setup instant
  • CSV personnel import with column mapping
  • Active dev cycle
  • Bus, ferry, train transfers added recently
  • Calendar feeds (Google/Apple/Outlook)
Weaknesses

Easy input.

The real adoption barrier isn't features — it's how painful it is to get data in. TMs default to Excel because Excel doesn't ask anything of them. Here are the input patterns the hub must nail, ranked by impact.

📎

Drag a PDF, get data

FOR → flights · riders · hotel confirmations · advance docs

The most powerful input pattern. Daysheets pioneered it for flights — drag up to 100 itinerary PDFs, AI parses every flight, passenger, locator, seat. User reviews side-by-side, clicks Import. The hub should extend this to every doc type.

1Drag PDF into hub
2AI parses structured fields
3System matches against existing records
4User reviews, fixes mismatches
5Import → data lands in right Day, right Show, right person

The extension nobody has built: apply this to riders. Drop in a rider PDF — AI extracts input list, stage plot positions, hospitality, schedule. User reviews and accepts.

📧

Forward an email, get a record

FOR → deal memos · venue confirmations · hotels · advance replies

Every TM gets a unique forwarding address. Forward any tour-related email → hub parses it. Deal memo → Show with venue, date, terms. Hotel confirmation → Hotel record on the right night. Venue advance reply → notes attached to that show.

1Forward email to tour-specific address
2AI classifies and parses
3System proposes records to create
4"Parsed 3 hotels and 1 flight. Review?"
5One-click approve, records created
📋

Import from a previous tour

FOR → past tour data already in the hub

Daysheets does this beautifully. New tour → "Import from Previous Tour" → pick which past tour, choose what to bring (roster, groups, tags, permissions, schedule templates, venues). Setup goes from 10 hours to 10 minutes.

What carries over: roster, roles, group structure, permission templates, schedule templates, custom field schemas. What doesn't: specific dates, hotels, flights — those would be wrong.

📊

CSV import with smart mapping

FOR → existing Excel sheets · agency routing exports · accounting workbooks

Most TMs and accountants start in Excel. The hub should accept any CSV, walk them through column mapping with smart defaults. Daysheets does this for personnel; the hub should do it for routing, hotels, rosters.

Wins: recognize column names ("Name," "Full Name," "First Name"). Auto-map common patterns. Preview with toggle-on/off per row before committing.

👥

Self-service profiles

FOR → the crew themselves

The TM shouldn't enter travel profiles. When a person is added to a group with "Request Information" on, they get an invite to fill in their own passport, frequent flyer, dietary, emergency contact, t-shirt size. Saves hours and the data is more accurate.

Bonus: auto-reminders for expiring passports and visas.

🤝

Vendors fill it in themselves

FOR → venue PMs · promoter reps · catering

The PM advances 50 venues today by sending 50 emails and typing replies into Master Tour. What if each venue PM got a magic link to a tailored form? Open link → fill in load-in time, parking, runner contact, hospital → submit → data lands in the Show record.

Shifts work from the tour to the venue (where the data already lives) and skips the transcription step. Builds the venue database over time as every venue self-enters.

📱

Mobile capture in the moment

FOR → things happening live, on the bus or backstage

One-tap captures: photo of receipt → categorized expense. Voice memo → transcribed note. Quick schedule edit with diff highlighting so the team sees what changed.

🔌

Direct integrations

FOR → third-party tools already in use

TripIt for travel. FlightAware for live flight tracking. Google/Outlook calendar feeds. Slack/iMessage forwarding for casual updates. QuickBooks/Xero for accounting export. Removes "but I already use X" objections.

Master Tour shipped in 2009 with manual entry as the only option. Daysheets made one ingest pattern (flight PDFs) magical. The next hub should make every data type that magical. Adoption follows ease.

For the head user (TM / PM)

Broadcast, don't type.

Stop thinking of the head user as a typist. They're a broadcaster and curator. They send one email to all venue contacts with a form link. They forward the agent's deal memo to a single inbox. They drop the rider PDF in once and it powers every advance. They press one button to push tomorrow's day sheet to 60 phones, each personalized.

The hub absorbs info from wherever it naturally lives, structures it, and redistributes it. Every minute saved on data entry turns a skeptic into a user.

Building the hub.

First, the information architecture: who owns what, who needs what, who's just aware. Then how info gets distributed. Then the build order.

Part one · the model

Three relationships to information.

Every role on a tour relates to every piece of data in one of four ways. Get this model right and permissions, push notifications, and day sheet personalization all fall out of it.

Owns

Source of truth

Creates and edits it. The system pulls updates from them. PM owns the show clock; travel agent owns flight bookings.

Needs

Required to work

Must have it to do their job. Push to them whenever it changes. Audio needs the input list; trucking needs the load-out time.

Sees

Peripheral awareness

Needs to know it exists. Timing only, not full details. Lighting sees video's build time so they don't conflict.

Blocked

Explicitly hidden

Not relevant or sensitive. Audio crew doesn't see artist's hotel. Most of the crew doesn't see settlement numbers.

Part two · how info moves

Four distribution patterns.

Information doesn't move the same way for every data type. The hub needs all four patterns, each tuned for a different kind of change.

Broadcast

All hands

Push to everyone. Used for tour-wide info: roster updates, venue changes, weather alerts, day-off announcements. The hub filters the message per role automatically but the trigger is one action.

TM updates Day 14 venue
→ Push fires to all 60 phones
→ Each one shows their personalized version of Day 14

Targeted push

By group

Only people who need it. Used for dept-specific changes: input list rev, lighting cue update, rigging plot revision. Members of other depts see nothing — no notification noise.

PM updates input list to rev 8
→ Push to Audio dept only
→ Lighting, video, security: silent

Peer-aware

Timing only

Adjacent depts see each other's build times to coordinate, but not the details. Lighting knows when audio finishes flying the PA. Audio knows when lighting finishes truss build. Neither sees the other's cue list.

Rigging marks "done at 09:30"
→ Lighting sees "Rigging done" timestamp
→ Audio sees "Rigging done" + can start fly
→ Neither sees rigging's hang point details

Self-serve pull

On demand

No push. People open the app and find what they need. Used for static reference: venue contacts, hospital address, hotel address, set list. Anything that doesn't change mid-day.

Driver lands at hotel
→ Opens app, taps "Hotel"
→ Sees address, parking, contact

The press block is tagged with the visibility "Artist, Publicist, Security, Mgmt." The system only notifies those four groups. The other 56 people on the tour never see the change — and that's the point. Notification fatigue is the death of these apps. Master Tour spams everyone; the hub doesn't.

Part four · easy distribution

Three head-user actions, fifty downstream effects.

The TM and PM should never feel like they're typing into a database. Three high-leverage interactions cover most of the work.

Action 1

"Apply Schedule Template"

One click. An "Arena Show Day" template loads load-in, soundcheck, doors, set times, load-out — all with default crew calls per dept. Personalizes to each person's role on save.

Action 2

"Publish Day Sheet"

One button. Locks in tomorrow's plan. Pushes 60 personalized notifications. Generates a redacted PDF for the backstage wall. Exports calendar items to anyone with feed subscriptions.

Action 3

"Send Advance"

One click per venue. Pulls the latest rider + plot + input list (always current). Generates the live link + QR. Emails the venue PM. Tracks read receipts and replies.

The principle: the head user authors and approves. The system distributes, filters, redacts, formats, and notifies. The TM should be able to run a 200-person tour from their phone — and on bad days, they will have to.
